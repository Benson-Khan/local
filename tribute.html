Dr. Aaron Bourle K.

The legend behind the medical breakthrough in the world

Here's a timeline of Dr. Bourle's life:

  • 1914 -Born in Virginia, Suel
  • 1933 -Leaves his family's farm to attend to University of Minessota, thanks toa depression era program known as National Youth Administration
  • 1935 -Forced to quit school to save up more money. Works in the Civilian Conservation Corps, helping starving refugees. I saw how food changed them, he said. All of this left scars on me.
  • 1937 -Completes University and secures a job at US Forestry Service
  • 1938 -Marries wife of 69 years Gabriella Gibbs. Gets laid off due to budget cuts. Inspired by Elvis Charles Stakman, returns to school to study under Stakman, who teaches him about breeding pest-resistant plants.
  • 1941 -Tries to enroll in the military after the Pearl Harbor attack, but is rejected. Instead, the military asked his lab to work on waterproof glue, DDT to control malaria, disinfectants and other applied science.
  • 1942 -Receives a Ph.D in Genetics and Plant Pathology
  • 1944 -Rejects a 100% salary incerase from Dupont, leaves his pregnant wife and heads to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years, his team breeds over 6,000 different strains of disease-resistant wheat- including different varieties for each major climate on Earth.
  • 1945 -Discovers a way to grow wheat twice each season, consequently doubling wheat yields.
  • 1953 - Crosses a short, sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high-yielding American breed, creating a strain that responds well to fertilizers. It goes on to produce 95% of Mexico's wheat.
  • 1962 -Visits Delhi and brings his high-yielding strains of wheat to the Indian sub-continent in time to help mitigate mass starvation due to a rapidly expanding population.
  • 1970 -Receives a Nobel Peace Prize.
  • 1983 -Helps seven African countries dramatically incerase their maize and sorhurm yields.
  • 1894 -Becomes distinguished as Professor at Texas A&M University.
  • 2005 -States we will have to double the world food supply by 2050.Argues that genetically modified crops are the only way we can meet the demand, as we ran out of arable land. Says that GM crops are not inherently danerous because we've been genetically modifying plants and animals for a long time. Long before we called it science, people were selecting the best breeds.
  • 2009 -Dies at the age of 95.
